About This Course

The ambitious goal of the students participating in the 'Autonomous Drones Elective' was to program a drone to fly autonomously within one week. The students with various backgrounds ‐ from neuroscience to electrical engineering ‐ learned within five days the basic concepts of Computer Vision, Object Recognition and Deep Learning in order to compete autonomously in a final drone race.

The course was supported by excellent lecturers to provide the necessary technical know-how and address the bigger picture of autonomous drone flight. The students ideated on drone related business cases in a Design Thinking workshop and discussed the advantages and risks that come with the usage of autonomous drones in a thought-provoking ethic session. Finally, each team developed a different approach to master an autonomous flight of their drone through a parkour of three small soccer gates. At the end of the weeks, the four teams competed in a final race.

Timeline

Each day of the week started off with an inspiring lecture given by an expert in the respective field. While all students possessed basic programming skills, the lectures covered a broad range of related fields necessary to understand the fundamental requirements and challenges of autonomous flying.

  • 8 Aug 2016

  • Kickoff

    Start of 2016 Autonomous Drone Elective

    The course was kicked off with a lecture from Patrick Christ giving an introduction to the project and the Python libraries available for drone control. The technical challenges of the first day were to get familiar with the drones, implement manual controls and extract camera frames. In the afternoon Laura Bechthold lead a thought provoking case study tackling ethical questions of drone usage and decision making.

    8 August 2016

  • Deep Learning Session

    Deep Learning lecture paired with hands on exercises

    On the second day Dr.Seyed-Ahmad Ahmadi held a lecture on Image Processing and Object Detection, which was complemented by an introduction to Deep Learning by Patrick Christ. After the theoretical input sessions, the students had to train a network to recognize hand written numbers using Nvidia's DIGIT System, before starting to implement their own classifier.

    9 August 2016

  • Autonomous Flying Attempts

    Hackathon and first autonomous flight attempts

    After a swift session on PID Controllers by Sebastian Schlecht the students spent their time putting the concepts they learned the previous days into practice. First autonomous flying attempts were made - with varied success.

    10 August 2016

  • Ideation Workshop

    Design Thinking workshop to ideate on business cases

    After half a day of further development and coding the students engaged in a Design Thinking workshop lead by Dr. Felix von Held and Felix Werle from the Institute for Innovation and Change Methodologies. In a structured approach the students ideated creative business cases related to autonomous drones and presented them in front of the class. As the deadline kept getting closer, the students optimized their algorithms in an all night long hackathon.

    11 August 2016

  • Race Day

    Last minute fixes with the finish line in sight

    The final day of 2016's drone elective was kicked off by a keynote speech presenting a current drone-related research project. Following that the entire team went to TUM's Informatics department in Garching to test the developed solutions by competing in a race through a small obstacle course.

    12 August 2016

Solutions

The students had a variety of study backgrounds which proved to be beneficial for the overall team work. In teams of two, the students decided on a specific approach to tackle the challenge. The insights from the lectures provided the necessary know-how and inspired each team to experiment with a different concept. While some teams decided to use the introduced deep learning concepts others relied on classic image recognition libraries. The entire code is open source and can be found on Github.

Finally, a race through a small obstacle parkour was organized to find which team implemented the most reliable solution. Three soccer gates served as the obstacles the drones had to cope with before passing the finish line. Each team had two attempts to finish the parkour as fast as possible.

EAGLE-EYE

Hagen Schmidtchen

Robotics, Cognition, Intelligence
Isabel Poppek

Management & Technology

Description

EAGLE-EYE combines the features of the low level control library libardrone with the marker tracking functionalities of OpenCV 3.1 and a software PID controller to achieve autonomous behavior. Once in autonomous mode, the drone follows the biggest visible ArUco marker by calculating movement commands from the position and the size of the biggest detected marker in every camera frame.

Description

P3N15 uses OpenCV filters to analyse the camera feed of the drone's front camera. When a marker is detected the drone tracks and follows the marker autonomously. Our custom marker detection is implemented using solely OpenCV. In order to avoid arbitrary objects to be recognized as markers, the symbol has to be recognized on a white area with DIN ratio.

The Center for Digital Technology Management (CDTM) is a joint institution of the Ludwig-Maximilians-University and the Technical University of Munich with a focus on innovation, product development and entrepreneurship. CDTM offers particularly talented students the interdisciplinary add-on study program ‘Technology Management’ which is a study program of the Elite Network of Bavaria. The study program is held in English and offers its students international and interdisciplinary course formats with hands-on experiences in cooperation with well-known industry partners. The study program consists of three semesters and one semester abroad. During those three semesters, students pass three core courses and their electives. Electives are designed to complement the core courses by offering students the opportunity to deepen their knowledge in an area of their choice.
